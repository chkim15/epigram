## How to Study: Review, Redo, and Retain

Completing a problem once is not the same as mastering it.
The candidates who perform best in quant interviews have not simply
seen the material, they have internalized it to the point where
the right technique surfaces instinctively under pressure.
The following practices are how that happens.

### Attempt first, always

Before reading any solution, spend real time on the problem. I mean
*a genuine attempt*, as if you are in the interview room.
*The struggle is not a detour from learning; it is the learning.*
A solution read without prior effort leaves almost no trace.
A solution read after genuine struggle restructures how you think about the entire topic.

The platform's **active learning mode** (toggle in your top-right profile menu) locks all solutions until you commit to an answer. Use it. Interviewers cannot give you the problem twice.

### Study the solution, not just the answer

When you check a solution, do not stop at confirming whether you were right.
Ask four questions:

1. **What was the key insight?** Identify it explicitly and write it down.
2. **Where exactly did my approach diverge?** Locate the precise step, not just the conclusion.
3. **What is the general pattern?** Most problems are instances of a small number of recurring techniques --- indicator variables, tail-sum expectation, Markov chain state equations, optimal stopping backward induction. Tag each problem to its pattern. You are building a mental library, not a list of answers.
4. **Can I explain this out loud in 60 seconds?** If not, you do not yet own the solution. The ability to verbalise a solution cleanly is exactly what the interview tests.

### Redo problems from scratch

Return to problems you have already solved --- ideally 3--7 days later ---
and redo them without looking at the solution.
This is spaced retrieval practice, the most effective known method
for building durable mathematical memory.

Focus your redo sessions on three categories:

- Problems you got wrong the first time.
- Problems where your approach was correct but slow or uncertain.
- Problems that felt easy --- these often conceal a subtlety interviewers deliberately probe.

### Get fluent with the techniques

Each topic is anchored by a small number of core techniques.
Before moving to the next topic, be able to:

- State the technique in one sentence without notes.
- Identify in under 30 seconds which technique applies to a new problem.
- Execute it correctly on a problem you have not seen before.

If you can only do the first --- you know the theory but not its application,
which is the most common gap interviewers expose.

### Use mock interviews as diagnostics

After each mock session, identify one specific thing to fix ---
a technique not retained, a problem type not seen, or a failure to think aloud under
time pressure. Each mock should generate a concrete action, not just a general impression.
