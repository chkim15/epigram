# Core Definitions

::: {.conceptbox title="Nash Equilibrium"}
A **Nash Equilibrium** is a strategy profile $(s_1^*,\ldots,s_n^*)$ such that no player can unilaterally increase their payoff: $$u_i(s_i^*, s_{-i}^*) \geq u_i(s_i, s_{-i}^*)
  \quad\text{for all }s_i,\;\text{for all }i$$

**Mixed strategy NE:** Players randomise. The key condition --- *each player is indifferent among all strategies in their support*: $$\text{If player }i\text{ mixes over }\{A,B\}:\quad
  u_i(A,\sigma_{-i}^*) = u_i(B,\sigma_{-i}^*)$$ **Existence:** Every finite game has at least one NE (possibly in mixed strategies) --- Nash's theorem.
:::

# Canonical Two-Player Games

::: {.workedbox number="1" difficulty="medium" problemid="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p124"}
**Prisoner's Dilemma: Analysis and Inefficiency.** Two suspects. Payoffs (years in prison, lower is better): cooperate/cooperate: (1,1); defect/cooperate: (0,3); cooperate/defect: (3,0); defect/defect: (2,2).

1.  Write the payoff matrix.

2.  Find all Nash equilibria.

3.  Why is the outcome Pareto-dominated?

4.  When might cooperation emerge?
:::

::: solutionbox
**(a) Payoff matrix** (entries: years for Player 1, Player 2 --- lower is better; rewrite as utility $=-\text{years}$):

|  | **Cooperate** | **Defect** |
|---|---|---|
| **Cooperate** | $(-1,-1)$ | $(-3,\phantom{-}0)$ |
| **Defect** | $(\phantom{-}0,-3)$ | $(-2,-2)$ |

**(b) Nash equilibrium.**

Defect is a **strictly dominant strategy** for each player: regardless of the opponent's action, Defect gives a strictly higher payoff (0 vs $-1$ against Cooperate; $-2$ vs $-3$ against Defect).

The unique NE is **(Defect, Defect)**, giving payoffs $(-2,-2)$.

**(c) Pareto inefficiency.** The outcome $(-2,-2)$ is Pareto-dominated by $(-1,-1)$ (mutual cooperation): both players would be strictly better off cooperating. The NE is inefficient because individual rationality leads to a collectively suboptimal outcome --- the essence of the "dilemma".

**(d) When does cooperation emerge?**

- **Repeated game (indefinite):** If the game is repeated infinitely (or with probability $\delta$ of continuation), the Folk Theorem says cooperation can be sustained as a NE for high enough $\delta$, via "grim trigger" or "tit-for-tat" strategies.

- **Communication / binding contracts:** If players can commit to cooperate before playing.

- **Altruism / social preferences:** If players care about each other's payoffs (non-standard preferences).

**Key takeaway:** The Prisoner's Dilemma illustrates that rational individual behaviour can be collectively irrational. Defect dominates $\Rightarrow$ unique NE $=$ (Defect, Defect) $\Rightarrow$ Pareto-dominated. Repeated interaction is the primary mechanism for sustaining cooperation.
:::

::: {.freeproblem number="2" difficulty="medium" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p125"}
**Battle of the Sexes: Multiple Equilibria.** Payoff matrix: (Opera,Opera): $(2,1)$; (Football,Football): $(1,2)$; (Opera,Football) or (Football,Opera): $(0,0)$.

1.  Find all pure strategy Nash equilibria.

2.  Find the mixed strategy Nash equilibrium.

3.  What is each player's expected payoff at the mixed NE?
:::

::: solutionbox
**(a) Pure strategy NE.**

Check each cell for mutual best responses:

- **(Opera, Opera):** Alice gets 2 (best against Bob=Opera); Bob gets 1 (best against Alice=Opera). **NE.**

- **(Football, Football):** Alice gets 1 (best against Bob=Football); Bob gets 2 (best against Alice=Football). **NE.**

- (Opera, Football), (Football, Opera): both give (0,0) --- either player would deviate unilaterally. Not NE.

**Two pure NE: (Opera, Opera) and (Football, Football).**

**(b) Mixed strategy NE.**

Let $p$ = Prob(Alice plays Opera) and $q$ = Prob(Bob plays Opera).

**Bob's mixing condition** (Alice must be indifferent): $$\mathbb{E}[\text{Alice}|\text{Opera}] = \mathbb{E}[\text{Alice}|\text{Football}]$$ $$2q + 0(1-q) = 0\cdot q + 1\cdot(1-q)
  \implies 2q = 1-q
  \implies q = \frac{1}{3}$$

**Alice's mixing condition** (Bob must be indifferent): $$1\cdot p + 0(1-p) = 0\cdot p + 2(1-p)
  \implies p = 2-2p
  \implies p = \frac{2}{3}$$

**Mixed NE:** Alice plays Opera with probability $2/3$; Bob plays Opera with probability $1/3$.

**(c) Expected payoffs at mixed NE.** $$\mathbb{E}[\text{Alice}] = 2\cdot\tfrac{2}{3}\cdot\tfrac{1}{3}
  + 0 + 0 + 1\cdot\tfrac{1}{3}\cdot\tfrac{2}{3}
  = \tfrac{4}{9}+\tfrac{2}{9} = \frac{2}{3}$$ $$\mathbb{E}[\text{Bob}] = 1\cdot\tfrac{2}{3}\cdot\tfrac{1}{3}
  + 0 + 0 + 2\cdot\tfrac{1}{3}\cdot\tfrac{2}{3}
  = \tfrac{2}{9}+\tfrac{4}{9} = \frac{2}{3}$$ Both players get expected payoff $2/3$ at the mixed NE --- *worse* than either pure NE payoff.

**Key takeaway:** Battle of the Sexes has 3 NE: two pure (players coordinate) and one mixed (players fail to coordinate with positive probability, getting lower expected payoff). The mixed NE is often dismissed as "less reasonable" because it yields lower payoffs than either pure NE.
:::

::: {.freeproblem number="3" difficulty="hard" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p126"}
**Cournot Duopoly.** Two firms, $P = 100-Q$, $Q=q_1+q_2$, zero marginal cost.

1.  Find each firm's best response function.

2.  Find the NE quantities and price.

3.  Compare to monopoly and perfect competition.
:::

::: solutionbox
**(a) Best response functions.**

Firm 1 maximises $\pi_1 = P\cdot q_1 = (100-q_1-q_2)q_1$. First-order condition: $\partial\pi_1/\partial q_1 = 100-2q_1-q_2=0$: $$q_1^*(q_2) = \frac{100-q_2}{2}$$ By symmetry: $q_2^*(q_1) = \dfrac{100-q_1}{2}$.

**(b) Nash equilibrium.**

Solving $q_1=q_2=q$ by symmetry: $q = (100-q)/2 \Rightarrow 2q=100-q \Rightarrow 3q=100$: $$\boxed{q_1^* = q_2^* = \frac{100}{3} \approx 33.3}$$ $$Q^* = \frac{200}{3},\qquad
  P^* = 100-\frac{200}{3} = \frac{100}{3}\approx33.3$$ $$\pi_i^* = P^*\cdot q_i^* = \frac{100}{3}\cdot\frac{100}{3}
  = \frac{10{,}000}{9} \approx 1{,}111$$

**(c) Comparison.**

| Outcome | $Q$ | $P$ | $\pi$ per firm |
|---|---|---|---|
| Monopoly ($Q=50$, $P=50$) | 50 | 50 | 1,250 |
| Cournot NE | $\approx 66.7$ | $\approx 33.3$ | $\approx 1{,}111$ |
| Perfect Competition ($P=0$) | 100 | 0 | 0 |

Cournot lies between monopoly (high price, low output) and perfect competition (zero price). Each firm would prefer to produce the monopoly share ($q=25$), but deviating to $q=100/3$ is rational since the best response to $q_j=25$ is $q_i=(100-25)/2=37.5>25$.

**Key takeaway:** Cournot NE: $q_i=a/(n+1)$, $P=a/(n+1)$ for $n$ symmetric firms with demand $P=a-Q$ and zero costs. As $n\to\infty$, the outcome approaches perfect competition.
:::

# Zero-Sum and Multi-Player Games

::: {.premiumproblem number="4" difficulty="medium" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p127"}
**Matching Pennies: No Pure Strategy NE.** Player 1 wins \$1 on match; Player 2 wins \$1 on mismatch.

1.  Show no pure strategy NE exists.

2.  Find the mixed strategy NE.

3.  What is each player's expected payoff?

4.  What general principle does this illustrate?
:::

::: solutionbox
**Payoff matrix** (Player 1's payoff; zero-sum so Player 2 gets the negative):

|  | **H** | **T** |
|---|---|---|
| **H** | $+1$ | $-1$ |
| **T** | $-1$ | $+1$ |

**(a) No pure strategy NE.**

- (H,H): Player 2 gets $-1$, deviates to T. Not NE.

- (H,T): Player 1 gets $-1$, deviates to T. Not NE.

- (T,H): Player 1 gets $-1$, deviates to H. Not NE.

- (T,T): Player 2 gets $-1$, deviates to H. Not NE.

No pure NE exists.

**(b) Mixed strategy NE.**

Let Player 1 play H with prob $p$. Player 2 is indifferent between H and T: $$\mathbb{E}_2[\text{H}] = \mathbb{E}_2[\text{T}]
  \implies -p + (1-p) = p - (1-p)
  \implies 1-2p = 2p-1 \implies p=\tfrac{1}{2}$$ By symmetry, Player 2 also plays H with $q=1/2$.

**Mixed NE: each player plays H and T each with probability $1/2$.**

**(c) Expected payoffs.** Each player's expected payoff $= 0$. (Zero-sum game with symmetric mixed NE: the value is 0.)

**(d) General principle.** Matching Pennies illustrates that **zero-sum games with no pure NE have a unique mixed NE**, given by the minimax strategy. By the **Minimax Theorem** (von Neumann 1928): every finite two-player zero-sum game has a value $v$ and optimal mixed strategies such that each player can guarantee at least $v$ (Player 1) or at most $v$ (Player 2) in expectation.

**Key takeaway:** In Matching Pennies, any predictable strategy is exploitable. The unique NE forces both players to be unpredictable (50/50). This underpins the value of randomisation in adversarial settings: poker, sports strategy, financial market making.
:::

::: {.premiumproblem number="5" difficulty="hard" problemId="sig_qdsi_p24"}
**Optimal Strategy in Weighted Rock-Paper-Scissors.** Winning by Rock $= +2$ points; winning by Paper or Scissors $= +1$ point. Tie $= 0$. Provide a mixed strategy that guarantees at least 0 expected points against any opponent.
:::

::: solutionbox
**Payoff matrix** (row player's score when winning; losses and ties $=0$):

|  | **R** | **P** | **S** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **R** | $0$ | $0$ | $2$ |
| **P** | $1$ | $0$ | $0$ |
| **S** | $0$ | $1$ | $0$ |

**Key:** winning with Rock $=2$; winning with Paper or Scissors $=1$; losing or tying $=0$.

**Expected payoff against each opponent pure action.** Let your mixed strategy be $(x,y,z)$ for (R, P, S): $$u(\text{vs R}) = y, \qquad u(\text{vs P}) = z, \qquad u(\text{vs S}) = 2x$$

**Minimax optimisation.** You want to maximise your guaranteed payoff: $$\max_{x+y+z=1,\;x,y,z\geq0}\;\min\{y,\;z,\;2x\}$$ At the optimum, all three quantities must be equal (otherwise shift weight to increase the minimum): $$y = z = 2x$$ With $x+y+z=1$: $x + 2x + 2x = 5x = 1 \Rightarrow x = 1/5$.

$$\boxed{x = \Pr(R) = \frac{1}{5},\quad
         y = \Pr(P) = \frac{2}{5},\quad
         z = \Pr(S) = \frac{2}{5}}$$

**Verify guaranteed payoff:** $u(\text{vs R}) = 2/5$,$u(\text{vs P}) = 2/5$,$u(\text{vs S}) = 2(1/5) = 2/5$.

Against any opponent mixed strategy, by linearity, your expected payoff is still $\geq 2/5$.

**Why does Rock get the lowest probability?** Rock has the highest reward ($+2$), which makes it attractive for the opponent to counter with Paper. To prevent the opponent from profitably playing Paper, Rock must be played *less* often. Counterintuitively, the strongest individual move gets the lowest weight.

$$\boxed{\text{Guaranteed expected score per round} = \frac{2}{5}}$$

**Key takeaway:** In an asymmetric zero-sum game, make the opponent indifferent by setting $u(\text{vs each pure}) = v$ (the game value). The minimax strategy here is $(1/5, 2/5, 2/5)$, not the naive $(1/3, 1/3, 1/3)$. Rock's higher reward means it must be played less frequently.
:::

::: {.premiumproblem number="6" difficulty="veryhard" problemId="game_theory_qdsi_p4"}
**Nash Equilibrium in the Smallest Unique Number Game.** Three players simultaneously choose a positive integer. A player wins iff they choose the *smallest* integer chosen by *exactly one* player. Find an optimal symmetric Nash equilibrium strategy.
:::

::: solutionbox
**Win condition.** You win iff you chose the *smallest* integer held by *exactly one* player. If you choose $k$, you win in exactly two mutually exclusive cases:

- **Case A:** Both other players chose $j>k$ --- then $k$ is unique and is the smallest.

- **Case B:** Both other players chose the *same* $j<k$ --- then $j$ is not unique (two players hold it), $k$ is unique, and no smaller unique exists.

**Win probability formula.** Let $F(k)=\sum_{j=1}^k q_j$ and $S(k-1)=\sum_{j=1}^{k-1}q_j^2$. Then: $$u(k) = \underbrace{(1-F(k))^2}_{\text{Case A}}
        + \underbrace{S(k-1)}_{\text{Case B}}$$

**Equilibrium condition.** In a symmetric NE, all $k$ in the support give equal win probability $v$: $u(k) = v$ for all $k$ with $q_k>0$.

The support must be infinite: if the support ended at $K$, choosing $K+1$ would win whenever the other two tie on any $j\leq K$ (Case B), giving $u(K+1)>u(K)$ --- profitable deviation. So every positive integer is played.

**Characterisation via tail sums.** Let $T_k = \sum_{j\geq k}q_j$ (tail probability, $T_1=1$). Note $1-F(k)=T_{k+1}$ and $S(k-1)=U_1-U_k$ where $U_k=\sum_{j\geq k}q_j^2$.

Taking $k\to\infty$ in $u(k)=v$ gives $v=U_1$. Substituting back: $$\boxed{U_k = T_{k+1}^2 \quad\text{for all }k\geq1}$$ This single identity fully characterises the NE distribution.

**Recursion for probabilities.** Since $q_k^2 = U_k - U_{k+1} = T_{k+1}^2 - T_{k+2}^2$ and $q_k = T_k - T_{k+1}$: $$T_{k+2} = \sqrt{T_{k+1}^2 - (T_k-T_{k+1})^2}, \qquad T_1=1$$ with $T_2$ determined by requiring $\sum q_k=1$, giving $T_2\approx0.5437$.

**First few equilibrium probabilities:**

| $k$ | $q_k$ | $k$ | $q_k$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | $\approx 0.4563$ | 6 | $\approx 0.0217$ |
| 2 | $\approx 0.2481$ | 7 | $\approx 0.0118$ |
| 3 | $\approx 0.1349$ | 8 | $\approx 0.0064$ |
| 4 | $\approx 0.0733$ | 9 | $\approx 0.0035$ |
| 5 | $\approx 0.0399$ | 10 | $\approx 0.0019$ |

**Equilibrium value.** Each player wins with probability $v = T_2^2 \approx (0.5437)^2 \approx 0.296$ at the NE. (Slightly less than $1/3$, since ties --- nobody wins --- occur with positive probability.)

**Key takeaway:** The symmetric NE is the unique distribution satisfying $U_k = T_{k+1}^2$. Play smaller numbers more often. The exact probabilities decrease faster than geometric (approximately $q_{k+1}/q_k \approx 0.54$ for small $k$, then faster). Do not play any fixed pure strategy --- any predictable choice is exploitable.
:::

# Technique Summary

::: {.techniquesummary title="Topic 23 Technique Toolkit"}

| Technique | Description |
|---|---|
| **Finding NE** | Pure: check each strategy profile for mutual best responses. Mixed: set opponents' payoffs equal (indifference). |
| **Dominant strategy** | If one strategy always dominates, it is played in every NE. Iterate elimination: IESDS. |
| **Zero-sum mixed NE** | Make the opponent indifferent. Minimax theorem guarantees existence. Game value $= 0$ if symmetric. |
| **Asymmetric payoffs** | Higher-reward actions get *lower* mixed probability (to discourage opponents from countering them). |
| **Cournot NE** | $q_i^* = a/((n+1)c)$ for linear demand $P=a-Q$, marginal cost $c$, $n$ symmetric firms. |
| **Battle of the Sexes** | 3 NE: 2 pure (coordinate), 1 mixed (payoff $=2/3$ each). Mixed NE payoff $<$ pure NE payoffs. |
| **Smallest unique number** | NE characterised by $U_k=T_{k+1}^2$. Probabilities decrease: $q_1\approx0.456$, $q_2\approx0.248$, ...; value $v\approx0.296$. |

:::
