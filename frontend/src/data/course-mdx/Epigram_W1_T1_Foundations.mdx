1.  **Set up any probability problem** from scratch --- define the sample space $\Omega$, identify the event $A$, and compute $\mathbb{P}(A)$ for uniform probability spaces.

2.  **Apply complementary counting** to handle "at least one" problems, including the Birthday Paradox and its generalisation.

3.  **Use the Inclusion-Exclusion Principle** to compute probabilities involving overlapping events and consecutive patterns.

6.  **Solve geometric probability problems** by mapping events onto regions and computing areas --- including chord intersection and semicircle containment.

7.  **Recognise and exploit symmetry** as the fastest path to an answer in interview settings.

*Problems 1--6 build all of the above skills. Problems 7--11 and A1--A2 are the level you need to crack final rounds at Citadel, Jane Street, and Two Sigma.*

# Introduction: What Is Probability?

Probability is the mathematics of uncertainty. In quantitative finance and trading, every model, every bet, and every risk estimate rests on probabilistic foundations. The goal of this topic is to build the *problem-solving toolkit* that lets you crack interview questions under pressure in 5--10 minutes.

::: {.conceptbox title="The Three Ingredients of Any Probability Problem"}
1.  **Sample space** $\Omega$: the set of all possible outcomes.

2.  **Event** $A \subseteq \Omega$: outcomes you care about.

3.  **Probability measure** $\mathbb{P}$: $\mathbb{P}(\Omega)=1$, $\mathbb{P}(\emptyset)=0$, countable additivity.

For **uniform** spaces: $\mathbb{P}(A) = |A|/|\Omega| = \text{favourable}/\text{total}$.
:::

**Interview strategy:** (1) Identify $\Omega$. (2) Count $|A|$ --- directly or via complementary counting / IEP. (3) Sanity-check boundary cases.

# Counting Fundamentals

## Permutations and Combinations

::: {.conceptbox title="Key Formulas"}
$$\begin{align*}
  \text{Permutations (ordered):}\quad
    & P(n,k) = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!} \\[4pt]
  \text{Combinations (unordered):}\quad
    & \dbinom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!\,(n-k)!}
\end{align*}$$ *Order matters $\Rightarrow$ permutation. Order doesn't $\Rightarrow$ combination.*
:::

## Warm-Up

::: {.workedbox number="1" difficulty="easy" problemid="citadel_qdsi_p28"}
**Majority Heads in Three Coin Tosses.** Flip a fair coin three times. What is the probability of getting more heads than tails?
:::

::: solutionbox
$|\Omega|=8$. Favourable (#H $\geq 2$): $\{$HHH, HHT, HTH, THH$\}$ = 4 outcomes. $$\mathbb{P} = \frac{4}{8} = \frac{1}{2}$$ **Slick:** Odd tosses $\Rightarrow$ no ties $\Rightarrow$ by symmetry $\mathbb{P}(\#H>\#T)=\mathbb{P}(\#T>\#H)=\tfrac{1}{2}$.

**Key takeaway:** For any odd number of fair coin tosses, majority heads has probability $\tfrac{1}{2}$ by symmetry --- no calculation needed.
:::

::: {.freeproblem number="2" difficulty="medium" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p40"}
**Parity of Heads in Coin Tosses.** Flip 10 fair coins. What is the probability the number of heads is even?

*Hint: try the algebraic trick $(p+(1-p))^n \pm ((1-p)-p)^n$.*
:::

::: solutionbox
**Method 1 --- Direct:** $|A|=\dbinom{10}{0}+\dbinom{10}{2}+\cdots+\dbinom{10}{10}=512$, $\mathbb{P}=512/1024=\tfrac{1}{2}$.

**Method 2 --- Algebraic trick:** $\mathbb{P}(\text{even heads}) = \dfrac{1+(1-2p)^n}{2}\Big|_{p=1/2} = \dfrac{1}{2}$ for all $n\geq1$.

**Method 3 --- Recurrence:** $a_n = \tfrac{1}{2}$ satisfies $a_n = p(1-a_{n-1})+(1-p)a_{n-1}$ when $a_{n-1}=\tfrac{1}{2}$.

**Key takeaway:** Parity of heads is always $\tfrac{1}{2}$ for any $n\geq1$ fair coin tosses.
:::

# Complementary Counting

::: {.techniquebox title="Complementary Counting"}
$\mathbb{P}(A)=1-\mathbb{P}(A^c)$. Use when "at least one" appears and the complement ("none" / "all different") is easier to count.
:::

::: {.freeproblem number="3" difficulty="medium" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p26"}
**The Birthday Paradox.** Assume 365 equally likely birthdays. Find the smallest $n$ such that $\mathbb{P}(\text{at least two people share a birthday})>\tfrac{1}{2}$.
:::

::: solutionbox
$\mathbb{P}(\text{all distinct})=\prod_{k=0}^{n-1}(1-k/365)$. Approximation $\ln(1-x)\approx-x$: threshold at $n(n-1)>730\ln2\approx506$, so $\mathbf{n=23}$.

**Key takeaway:** Pairs grow as $\dbinom{n}{2}$, reaching $\sim365$ when $n\approx23$. General threshold: $n\approx1.18\sqrt{m}$ for pool size $m$.
:::

# Combinatorial Techniques

::: {.techniquebox title="Non-Adjacent Selection"}
Choose $k$ from $\{1,\ldots,n\}$ with no two adjacent: $\dbinom{n-k+1}{k}$ ways. (Substitute $y_i=x_i-(i-1)$ to absorb gap constraints.)
:::

::: {.freeproblem number="4" difficulty="medium" problemId="citadel_qdsi_p26"}
**Probability of Choosing Three Non-Consecutive Integers.** From $\{1,\ldots,10\}$, pick 3 distinct numbers uniformly at random. What is the probability no two are adjacent?
:::

::: solutionbox
Total: $\dbinom{10}{3}=120$. Favourable: $\dbinom{8}{3}=56$. $\mathbb{P} = 56/120 = \mathbf{7/15}$.

**Key takeaway:** Memorise $\dbinom{n-k+1}{k}$ --- it turns a constrained adjacency problem into an unconstrained one.
:::

# Inclusion-Exclusion Principle

::: {.conceptbox title="Inclusion-Exclusion Principle (IEP)"}
$$\mathbb{P}\!\Bigl(\bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i\Bigr)
  = \sum_i\mathbb{P}(A_i) - \sum_{i<j}\mathbb{P}(A_i\cap A_j)
  + \sum_{i<j<k}\mathbb{P}(A_i\cap A_j\cap A_k) - \cdots$$ Signal phrase: **"at least one"**.
:::

::: {.freeproblem number="5" difficulty="medium" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p3"}
**Probability of Consecutive Sixes in Three Dice Rolls.** Roll a fair six-sided die 3 times. What is the probability of getting at least one occurrence of the consecutive pattern $66$?
:::

::: solutionbox
Let $A=\{\text{rolls 1,2 both 6}\}$, $B=\{\text{rolls 2,3 both 6}\}$. $$\mathbb{P}(A\cup B)=\frac{1}{36}+\frac{1}{36}-\frac{1}{216}=\frac{11}{216}\approx5.1\%$$ **Key takeaway:** Define $A_i=\{\text{pattern starts at }i\}$, apply IEP. Intersection $A_i\cap A_{i+1}$ is the longer overlapping pattern.
:::

# Circular Arrangements

::: {.conceptbox title="Circular Permutations"}
Fix one person: $(n-1)!$ total. Block of $k$ friends: $(n-k)!\cdot k!$ arrangements. $$\mathbb{P}(\text{$k$ friends consecutive}) = \frac{k!\,(n-k)!}{(n-1)!}$$
:::

::: {.freeproblem number="6" difficulty="medium" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p70"}
**Adjacent Friends Around a Round Table.** **Part 1.** 5 people, 2 friends. $\mathbb{P}(\text{friends adjacent})$? **Part 2.** Generalise to $n$ people, $k$ friends all consecutive.
:::

::: solutionbox
**Part 1.** Fix one friend; 4 positions, 2 adjacent: $\mathbb{P}=\mathbf{1/2}$.

**Part 2.** $\mathbb{P} = \dfrac{k!\,(n-k)!}{(n-1)!}$. Verify $n=5, k=2$: $12/24=1/2$.

**Key takeaway:** Always fix one person first. The block trick handles all consecutive-group problems.
:::

# Advanced Counting and Geometric Probability

## Card Dealing

::: {.premiumproblem number="7" difficulty="hard" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p17"}
**Probability Each Player Receives an Ace.** A 52-card deck is dealt to 4 players (13 cards each). What is the probability every player receives at least one ace?
:::

::: solutionbox
**Step 1: Total number of equally likely deals.** A deal assigns 13 cards to each of 4 labeled players: $$|\Omega| = \binom{52}{13}\binom{39}{13}\binom{26}{13}\binom{13}{13}
  = \frac{52!}{(13!)^4}$$

**Step 2: Each player must receive exactly 1 ace.** There is no other way to give all 4 players at least one ace (only 4 aces exist). The number of ways to assign the 4 distinct aces to the 4 labeled players is: $$4!$$

**Step 3: Deal the remaining 48 non-ace cards.** Each player already has 1 ace and needs 12 more cards from the 48 non-aces: $$\binom{48}{12}\binom{36}{12}\binom{24}{12}\binom{12}{12}
  = \frac{48!}{(12!)^4}$$

**Step 4: Favorable deals.** $$|\text{favorable}| = 4!\cdot\frac{48!}{(12!)^4}$$

**Step 5: Compute the probability.** $$\mathbb{P}(\text{each player has an ace})
  = \frac{4!\cdot\dfrac{48!}{(12!)^4}}{\dfrac{52!}{(13!)^4}}
  = 4!\cdot\frac{48!}{52!}\cdot\frac{(13!)^4}{(12!)^4}$$

**Step 6: Simplify.** $$\frac{48!}{52!} = \frac{1}{52\cdot51\cdot50\cdot49},
  \qquad
  \left(\frac{13!}{12!}\right)^4 = 13^4$$ $$\mathbb{P}(\text{each player has an ace})
  = \frac{4!\cdot 13^4}{52\cdot51\cdot50\cdot49}$$ Using $52 = 4\cdot13$ to cancel with $4! = 24$: $$= \frac{24\cdot13^4}{4\cdot13\cdot51\cdot50\cdot49}
  = \frac{6\cdot13^3}{51\cdot50\cdot49}$$

$$\boxed{
  \mathbb{P}(\text{each player has an ace})
  = \frac{24\cdot 13^4}{52\cdot 51\cdot 50\cdot 49}
  = \frac{6\cdot 13^3}{51\cdot 50\cdot 49}
  = \frac{2197}{20825}
  \approx 10.5\%
  }$$

**Key takeaway:** The direct-counting approach --- assign aces first, then deal non-aces --- is cleaner than IEP here. Despite 4 aces for 4 players, the probability each receives one is only $\approx 1$ in $9.5$, because most deals concentrate two or more aces in the same hand.
:::

## Derangements

::: {.keyresult title="Derangement Formula"}
$D_n = n!\sum_{k=0}^{n}(-1)^k/k!\approx n!/e$. For $n\geq5$: $\mathbb{P}(\text{derangement})\approx1/e\approx36.8\%$.
:::

::: {.premiumproblem number="8" difficulty="medium" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p25"}
**The Derangement Problem.** 5 firms, 5 cover letters placed randomly. Probability no letter goes to the correct firm?
:::

::: solutionbox
$$\mathbb{P} = \sum_{k=0}^{5}\frac{(-1)^k}{k!}
  = 1-1+\tfrac{1}{2}-\tfrac{1}{6}+\tfrac{1}{24}-\tfrac{1}{120}
  = \frac{44}{120}=\frac{11}{30}\approx36.7\%$$ **Key takeaway:** $\approx1/e$ for $n\geq5$. Recurrence: $D_n=(n-1)(D_{n-1}+D_{n-2})$.
:::

## Geometric Probability

::: {.techniquebox title="Geometric Probability"}
$\mathbb{P}(A)=\text{measure of favourable region}/\text{total measure}$. Identify the right sample space, map the event geometrically, then compute. **Always draw the picture.**
:::

::: {.premiumproblem number="9" difficulty="hard" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p24"}
**Probability All Random Points Lie in a Semicircle.** Pick $N$ points uniformly on a circle's circumference. What is the probability all $N$ lie within some semicircle?
:::

::: solutionbox
Let $A_i=\{\text{all other }N-1\text{ points in the semicircle clockwise from }i\}$. Events are mutually exclusive, $\mathbb{P}(A_i)=(1/2)^{N-1}$. $$\boxed{\mathbb{P} = \frac{N}{2^{N-1}}}$$ **Key takeaway:** Mutual exclusivity of $A_1,\ldots,A_N$ is the key step.
:::

::: {.premiumproblem number="10" difficulty="hard" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p10"}
**Probability Two Random Chords Intersect.** Choose chords by picking four endpoints uniformly on a circle. What is the probability the chords intersect?
:::

:::: solutionbox
4 random points $\to$ 3 equally likely pairings. Chords intersect iff endpoints alternate; exactly 1 of 3 pairings does this. $$\boxed{\mathbb{P} = \frac{1}{3}}$$

::: {.warningbox title=""}
Different chord-sampling methods give different answers (Bertrand's paradox). Always clarify the sampling method in an interview.
:::
::::

::: {.premiumproblem number="11" difficulty="veryhard" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p86"}
**Triangle Formation from Random Stick Breaks.** **(1)** Unit stick cut at two uniform points. $\mathbb{P}(\text{triangle})$? **(2)** First cut uniform; break the longer piece at a uniform point. $\mathbb{P}(\text{triangle})$?
:::

::: solutionbox
**(1)** Event = each piece $<1/2$ = two triangles in $[0,1]^2$, total area $1/4$. $\boxed{\mathbb{P}_1=1/4}$.

**(2)** Condition on first cut $U\sim\mathrm{Unif}(0,\tfrac{1}{2})$, second $V\sim\mathrm{Unif}(U,1)$. Need $V>1/2$ and $V<U+1/2$: $$\mathbb{P}_2 = 2\int_0^{1/2}\frac{U}{1-U}\,dU = 2\ln2-1\approx38.6\%$$ **Key takeaway:** Breaking the longer piece raises the probability from 25% to $\approx$`<!-- -->`{=html}38.6%.
:::

# Technique Summary

::: {.techniquesummary title="Topic 1 Technique Toolkit"}

| Technique | Description |
|---|---|
| **Uniform probability** | Count favourable / count total. |
| **Complementary** | $\mathbb{P}(A)=1-\mathbb{P}(A^c)$. Use for "at least one". |
| **Symmetry** | Fair coin/dice: use symmetry before computing. |
| **IEP** | $\mathbb{P}(\bigcup A_i)=\sum\mathbb{P}(A_i)-\sum\mathbb{P}(A_i\cap A_j)+\cdots$ |
| **Non-adjacent** | $k$ from $n$, no two adjacent: $\dbinom{n-k+1}{k}$. |
| **Circular** | Fix one; block of $k$: $(n-k)!\cdot k!$. |
| **Derangement** | $\mathbb{P}\approx1/e\approx36.8\%$ for $n\geq5$. |
| **Geometric** | Map to region; compute area. Draw it. |
| **Chord intersection** | 4 pts $\to$ 3 pairings, 1 crosses: $\mathbb{P}=1/3$. |
| **Semicircle containment** | $\mathbb{P}=N/2^{N-1}$. |

:::
