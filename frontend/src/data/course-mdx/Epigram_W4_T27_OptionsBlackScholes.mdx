# No-Arbitrage Pricing

::: {.conceptbox title="Key Relationships"}
**Put-Call Parity (European options):** $$C - P = S - Ke^{-rT}$$

**Risk-neutral pricing (binomial tree):** $$p^* = \frac{e^{rT} - d}{u - d}
  \qquad
  V_0 = e^{-rT}\bigl[p^*\,V_u + (1-p^*)\,V_d\bigr]$$

**Replicating portfolio:** $\Delta = (V_u-V_d)/(S_u-S_d)$ (shares), $B = e^{-rT}(V_d\,S_u-V_u\,S_d)/(S_u-S_d)$ (bond).
:::

::: {.workedbox number="1" difficulty="medium" problemid="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p136"}
**Put-Call Parity and Arbitrage.** European call: $S=\$100$, $K=\$105$, $r=5\%$/year, $T=1$ yr, $C=\$8$.

1.  State Put-Call Parity.

2.  Find the fair put price.

3.  If the put is priced at \$10, construct an explicit arbitrage portfolio.
:::

::: solutionbox
**(a) Put-Call Parity.** For European options on a non-dividend-paying stock: $$C - P = S - Ke^{-rT}$$ This follows from no-arbitrage: both sides replicate a forward contract with delivery price $K$.

**(b) Fair put price.** $$P = C - S + Ke^{-rT}
  = 8 - 100 + 105e^{-0.05}
  = 8 - 100 + 99.88
  = \boxed{\$7.88}$$

**(c) Arbitrage when $P=\$10$.**

The put is overpriced ($10 > 7.88$). Sell the overpriced put and hedge:

| Action | Today | $S_T > K$ | $S_T \leq K$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sell put | $+10$ | $0$ | $-(K-S_T)$ |
| Buy call | $-8$ | $+(S_T-K)$ | $0$ |
| Short stock | $+100$ | $-S_T$ | $-S_T$ |
| Lend $Ke^{-rT}=99.88$ | $-99.88$ | $+K$ | $+K$ |
| **Net** | **$+2.12$** | $0$ | $0$ |

Risk-free profit today: $10 - 8 + 100 - 99.88 = \boxed{\$2.12}$, with zero payoff at expiry in both scenarios.

**Key takeaway:** Put-Call Parity: $C-P=S-Ke^{-rT}$. If violated, sell the overpriced side and buy the underpriced side plus a replicating hedge. Profit is locked in today.
:::

::: {.freeproblem number="2" difficulty="medium" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p137"}
**One-Step Binomial Tree.** Stock at \$50; up to \$65 or down to \$40 in 1 year; $r=5\%$.

1.  Find risk-neutral probabilities $p^*$, $1-p^*$.

2.  Price a European call with $K=\$55$.

3.  Verify via replicating portfolio.
:::

::: solutionbox
**(a) Risk-neutral probability.**

With discrete discounting, $(1+r)=1.05$. Set $(1+r)S_0 = p^* S_u + (1-p^*)S_d$: $$1.05\times50 = 65p^* + 40(1-p^*)
  \implies 52.5 = 40 + 25p^*
  \implies p^* = \frac{12.5}{25} = \boxed{0.5}$$

**(b) Call price.** Payoffs: $C_u = \max(65-55,0)=10$, $C_d = \max(40-55,0)=0$. $$C_0 = \frac{1}{1.05}\bigl[0.5\times10 + 0.5\times0\bigr]
  = \frac{5}{1.05}
  \approx \boxed{\$4.76}$$

**(c) Replicating portfolio ($\Delta$ shares $+$ bond $B$).**

Require $\Delta S_u + 1.05B = C_u$ and $\Delta S_d + 1.05B = C_d$: $$65\Delta + 1.05B = 10,\qquad 40\Delta + 1.05B = 0$$ Subtracting: $25\Delta = 10 \Rightarrow \Delta = \dfrac{10}{25} = 0.40$ shares.

From the down equation: $1.05B = -16 \Rightarrow B = -\dfrac{16}{1.05} \approx -\$15.24$.

Portfolio value today: $$V_0 = 0.4\times50 - \frac{16}{1.05} = 20 - 15.238 = \boxed{\$4.76}$$  matches part (b) by no-arbitrage.

Verify payoffs:

- Up: $0.4\times65 + 1.05\times(-16/1.05) = 26 - 16 = \$10$

- Down: $0.4\times40 + 1.05\times(-16/1.05) = 16 - 16 = \$0$

**Key takeaway:** Risk-neutral pricing and replicating portfolio always give the same answer by no-arbitrage. $\Delta=0.40$ means you hold 40% of a share to hedge 1 call.
:::

::: {.freeproblem number="3" difficulty="hard" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p138"}
**Two-Step Binomial Tree.** $S_0=\$50$, $u=1.2$, $d=0.85$, $r=3\%$ per period.

1.  Draw the price tree for two periods.

2.  Find risk-neutral probability $p^*$.

3.  Price a European put with $K=\$52$.

4.  Price an American put with $K=\$52$. Does early exercise ever make sense?
:::

::: solutionbox
**(a) Price tree.**

$S_{uu}=50\times1.44=72.00$, $S_{ud}=50\times1.02=51.00$, $S_{dd}=50\times0.7225=36.12$.

**(b) Risk-neutral probability.** $$p^* = \frac{(1+r) - d}{u - d}
  = \frac{1.03 - 0.85}{1.2 - 0.85}
  = \frac{0.18}{0.35}
  = \boxed{\frac{18}{35}\approx0.5143}$$

**(c) European put ($K=\$52$).**

Terminal payoffs: $P_{uu}=0$, $P_{ud}=1$, $P_{dd}=52-36.125=15.875$.

Period-1 values (discount by $1/(1+r)=1/1.03$): $$P_u = \frac{1}{1.03}\!\left(\tfrac{18}{35}\times0
        + \tfrac{17}{35}\times1\right)
  = \frac{17}{35\times1.03} \approx \$0.4716$$ $$P_d = \frac{1}{1.03}\!\left(\tfrac{18}{35}\times1
        + \tfrac{17}{35}\times15.875\right)
  = \frac{8.225}{1.03} \approx \$7.985$$

Period-0: $$P_0 = \frac{1}{1.03}\!\left(\tfrac{18}{35}\times0.4716
        + \tfrac{17}{35}\times7.985\right)
  \approx \frac{4.121}{1.03}
  \approx \boxed{\$4.00}$$

**(d) American put.**

At each node, take $\max(\text{intrinsic value},\;\text{continuation value})$.

*Up node ($S_u=60$):* Intrinsic $=\max(52-60,0)=0$. Continuation $=0.4716 > 0$. Do not exercise: $P_u^{\text{Am}}=0.4716$.

*Down node ($S_d=42.50$):* Intrinsic $=52-42.5=9.50$. Continuation $=7.985 < 9.50$. **Early exercise is optimal**: $P_d^{\text{Am}}=9.50$.

*Time 0:* Intrinsic $=52-50=2$. Continuation value: $$P_0^{\text{cont}} = \frac{1}{1.03}\!\left(\tfrac{18}{35}\times0.4716
  + \tfrac{17}{35}\times9.50\right)
  \approx \frac{4.857}{1.03} \approx 4.715$$ Since $4.715 > 2$, do not exercise at time 0. $$\boxed{P_0^{\text{Am}} \approx \$4.72}$$

The American put is worth more than the European put (\$4.72 vs \$4.00) because early exercise at the down node is optimal --- intrinsic value \$9.50 exceeds the continuation value \$7.99.

**Key takeaway:** American puts can be worth more than European puts due to early exercise. Early exercise is rational when the intrinsic value exceeds the continuation (time) value --- typically for deep in-the-money puts when interest rates are positive.
:::

# Hedging, Black-Scholes, and Market Realities

::: {.premiumproblem number="4" difficulty="medium" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p139"}
**Delta Hedging Intuition.** You sold 1 call with $\Delta=0.6$ on a stock at \$100.

1.  How many shares to hold for Delta-neutrality?

2.  If stock rises \$1, how does the hedged portfolio change?

3.  What does $\Gamma=0.05$ tell you?

4.  Explain the Gamma--Theta trade-off for a long option.
:::

::: solutionbox
**(a) Delta-neutral hedge.**

You are short 1 call with $\Delta=0.6$. Buy $\Delta=0.6$ shares of stock. Your portfolio: $+0.6$ shares $-1$ call.

Portfolio Delta: $0.6\times1 - 0.6 = 0$. Delta-neutral.

**(b) Portfolio change for $\Delta S = +\$1$.**

Using the Delta approximation:

- Short call changes by $\approx -\Delta\cdot\Delta S = -0.6\times1 = -\$0.60$.

- Long 0.6 shares change by $+0.6\times1 = +\$0.60$.

Net change: $$\Delta V_{\text{portfolio}} \approx -0.60 + 0.60 = \boxed{\$0}$$ This is *exactly* what Delta-neutrality means: to first order, small stock price moves do not change the portfolio value.

**Note on second-order effects:** For large moves, the Gamma of the short call ($-0.05$) causes a *second-order* loss of $\frac{1}{2}\times0.05\times(\Delta S)^2$. But for a \$1 move, this is only $\approx\$0.025$ --- small relative to the first-order effect that exactly cancels.

**(c) Gamma $=0.05$.**

Gamma measures the rate of change of Delta with respect to the stock price: $$\Gamma = \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial S^2} = 0.05$$ If the stock rises by \$1, the call's Delta increases by approximately $0.05$: from 0.60 to 0.65. You must *rebalance* by buying $0.05$ more shares to remain Delta-neutral.

**(d) Gamma--Theta trade-off.**

For a **long option** position:

- **Gamma** $>0$: you benefit from large stock moves in either direction (convexity). A big move up or down increases your P&L.

- **Theta** $<0$: you lose value as time passes (time decay). Every day you hold the option, it decays.

The Black-Scholes PDE enforces: $$\Theta + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \Gamma + rS\Delta = rV$$ For an at-the-money option: $\Theta \approx -\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\Gamma$. A long position *pays* Theta (time decay) in exchange for *collecting* Gamma (profit from realised moves). If realised volatility exceeds implied volatility, the Gamma gains exceed the Theta cost: the position profits.

**Key takeaway:** Delta-neutral: hold $\Delta$ shares per short call. Gamma measures how fast Delta changes --- large Gamma means frequent rebalancing needed. Long options: pay Theta, receive Gamma.
:::

::: {.premiumproblem number="5" difficulty="hard" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p140"}
**Black-Scholes: Assumptions, PDE, and Greeks.**

1.  State the five key assumptions.

2.  Write the Black-Scholes PDE.

3.  For a long European call: sign and intuition for each Greek.

4.  Behaviour of Greeks deep ITM vs deep OTM.
:::

::: solutionbox
**(a) Black-Scholes assumptions.**

1.  Stock price follows geometric Brownian motion: $dS = \mu S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW_t$.

2.  Constant volatility $\sigma$ and risk-free rate $r$.

3.  No dividends during the option's life.

4.  No transaction costs or taxes; continuous trading possible.

5.  No arbitrage opportunities.

**(b) Black-Scholes PDE.** $$\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\,\frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial S^2}
  + rS\,\frac{\partial C}{\partial S}
  + \frac{\partial C}{\partial t}
  = rC$$

**(c) Greeks for a long European call.**

| Greek | Sign | Intuition |
|---|---|---|
| $\Delta = \partial C/\partial S$ | $+$ (0 to 1) | Call value rises when stock rises. |
| $\Gamma = \partial^2 C/\partial S^2$ | $+$ | Delta rises when stock rises (convexity). Call is always convex in $S$. |
| $\Theta = \partial C/\partial t$ | $-$ | Time decay: option loses value as expiry approaches (all else equal). |
| $\mathcal{V} = \partial C/\partial \sigma$ (Vega) | $+$ | Higher volatility increases the probability of large favourable moves; call is worth more. |
| $\rho = \partial C/\partial r$ | $+$ | Higher rates reduce the PV of the strike payment; the call is worth more. |

**(d) Deep ITM vs deep OTM.**

| Greek | Deep ITM ($S\gg K$) | Deep OTM ($S\ll K$) |
|---|---|---|
| $\Delta$ | $\approx 1$ (acts like stock) | $\approx 0$ (insensitive) |
| $\Gamma$ | $\approx 0$ | $\approx 0$ |
| $\Gamma$ (ATM) | maximised at-the-money | maximised at-the-money |
| $\Theta$ | large negative | small negative |
| Vega | $\approx 0$ | $\approx 0$ |
| Vega (ATM) | maximised at-the-money | maximised at-the-money |

Deep ITM calls behave like stock ($\Delta\to1$). Deep OTM calls are nearly worthless and insensitive to small moves. Gamma and Vega are both maximised at-the-money where uncertainty about expiry outcome is highest.

**Key takeaway:** BS PDE: $\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2S^2\Gamma
+ rS\Delta + \Theta = rC$. Call Greeks: $\Delta>0$, $\Gamma>0$, $\Theta<0$, Vega$>0$, $\rho>0$. Gamma and Vega peak ATM.
:::

::: {.premiumproblem number="6" difficulty="medium" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p141"}
**Volatility Smile and Market Reality.**

1.  What is the volatility smile/smirk?

2.  Why do equity index puts have higher implied vol?

3.  Name two model extensions that capture the smile.
:::

::: solutionbox
**(a) Volatility smile/smirk.**

In the Black-Scholes model, all options on the same underlying with the same expiry should have the same implied volatility $\sigma$ (since $\sigma$ is a constant input). In practice, if you back out the implied volatility from market prices, it varies with strike $K$:

- **Volatility smile:** Implied vol is higher for both low and high strikes (U-shaped vs ATM). Common in FX markets.

- **Volatility skew/smirk:** Implied vol is monotonically higher for lower strikes. Typical in equity index markets.

This violates the BS assumption of constant $\sigma$, revealing that the model misprices options at extreme strikes.

**(b) Equity index skew: tail risk.**

For equity indices (e.g. S&P 500), deep out-of-the-money puts (low strike) trade at *higher* implied volatility than ATM options. This reflects:

- **Demand for crash protection:** Portfolio managers buy OTM puts to hedge against large market declines, bidding up their prices (and implied vol).

- **Fat left tail / negative skewness:** Markets have historically crashed (fallen sharply) more often than they have "crashed upward". The risk-neutral distribution implied by option prices has a fatter left tail than the lognormal assumed by Black-Scholes.

**(c) Model extensions.**

1.  **Stochastic volatility** (e.g. Heston 1993): $\sigma$ itself follows a mean-reverting process. Captures the smile because volatility clustering generates fat tails. The Heston model has a closed-form solution for European options.

2.  **Local volatility** (Dupire 1994 / Derman-Kani): $\sigma = \sigma(S,t)$ is a deterministic function of stock price and time, calibrated to match all market prices exactly. Captures the smile by construction, but has poor dynamics for forward volatility.

3.  **Jump-diffusion** (Merton 1976): adds Poisson jumps to the GBM. Sudden large drops generate a left tail fatter than lognormal, producing a skew.

**Key takeaway:** Volatility smile $=$ implied vol varies with strike, violating BS. Equity skew: OTM puts expensive due to crash demand and fat left tail. Extensions: stochastic vol (Heston), local vol (Dupire), jump-diffusion (Merton).
:::

# Technique Summary

::: {.techniquesummary title="Topic 27 Technique Toolkit"}

| Technique | Description |
|---|---|
| **Put-Call Parity** | $C - P = S - Ke^{-rT}$. Violation $\Rightarrow$ sell overpriced side, buy underpriced plus hedge. |
| **Risk-neutral prob.** | $p^* = (e^{rT}-d)/(u-d)$. Discount at $r$, not $\mu$. |
| **Replicating portfolio** | $\Delta = (V_u-V_d)/(S_u-S_d)$; bond $B$ locks in value. |
| **American vs European put** | American $\geq$ European. Early exercise: intrinsic $>$ continuation value (deep ITM, high $r$). |
| **Delta hedge** | Hold $\Delta$ shares per short call. Rebalance as $\Delta$ drifts (Gamma effect). |

| **Vol smile** | Equity skew: OTM puts expensive. Models: stochastic vol (Heston), local vol (Dupire), jump-diffusion. |
