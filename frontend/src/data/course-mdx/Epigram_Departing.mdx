# Interview Day: Final Remarks

<GoldBox>

You have put in the work. You have seen the problems, understood the techniques,
and built the intuition. What follows is not more preparation --- it is a reminder
of what to do with it when you are in the room.

Technical skill is necessary. It is not sufficient.
The candidates who get offers are the ones who can also communicate their thinking
clearly, handle themselves well in the opening minutes, and leave a strong impression
at the close. None of this is difficult --- it just requires the same deliberate
preparation you gave the mathematics.

</GoldBox>

## The Opening Minutes

Most quant interviews begin with 2--3 minutes of conversation before any technical
problem is posed. This is not small talk --- it is part of the evaluation.
Interviewers form strong impressions early and those impressions colour
everything that follows. Do not leave the opening unscripted.

### Tell me about yourself

Prepare a 90-second version: who you are, what you studied, and why you are specifically
pursuing quant finance. The structure that works:

1. **Background in one sentence.** Degree, institution, relevant focus.
2. **The pivot.** A concrete moment or experience that directed you toward quantitative trading or research --- not a generic claim about loving markets or maths, but a specific story.
3. **Why this firm.** One genuine, specific reason. Research their trading strategies, recent hires, or published work if available.

Vague motivation answers like *"I have always been interested in markets"*
signal low self-awareness and low preparation. Specificity signals both.
Practise this out loud until it sounds natural, not rehearsed.

### Conveying genuine enthusiasm

Interviewers at top firms choose between candidates who are all technically strong.
Genuine enthusiasm for the work --- for the mathematical depth of it, not just the
compensation --- is a meaningful differentiator. Let it show. Ask a follow-up question
about the interviewer's own work if the opening allows it.

## The Resume Walkthrough

At some point in most interviews, the interviewer will say:
"Walk me through your resume" or "Tell me more about this project."
This is not a courtesy question. It is frequently the entry point to
the hardest technical questions in the interview.

<GrayBox>

**The rule:** *know every line on your resume well enough to go three levels deep.*
If it says *stochastic processes*, be ready to state and prove the optional
stopping theorem. If it says *machine learning*, be ready for the bias-variance
decomposition from first principles. If it says *quantitative research*, be ready to describe your methodology, your results, and their limitations under sustained questioning.

</GrayBox>

Do not list anything you cannot defend. A line you cannot defend
under pressure is worse than a shorter resume.

For any research or project experience, prepare a two-minute verbal summary
with three components: the problem you were solving, your specific contribution
(not the team's), and the key quantitative result. Numbers matter ---
"improved prediction accuracy by 12%" is remembered; "improved the model" is not.

## Thinking Out Loud

Silence is the most common and most costly mistake candidates make in quant interviews.

Interviewers are not only evaluating whether you reach the correct answer.
They are evaluating how you think --- your instincts, your self-correction,
your ability to reason under uncertainty. A candidate who narrates clear,
structured reasoning and reaches a wrong answer will often advance over a candidate
who produces the correct answer in silence. The reasoning is the signal.

### What this looks like in practice

- **State your assumptions explicitly** before you begin. If you assume the coin is fair, say so. If you assume a discrete state space, say so. This both clarifies your thinking and gives the interviewer the opportunity to correct a misunderstanding early.
- **Name the technique before you apply it.** "I think this is a linearity-of-expectation problem. Let me define indicators for each event" is far more valuable to an interviewer than a silent derivation that arrives at the right number.
- **Estimate before you compute.** For any numerical result, say what you expect the answer to be in order of magnitude before working through the algebra. This demonstrates physical intuition and gives you a sanity check.
- **When stuck, narrate the stuck.** "I know the stationary distribution satisfies $\pi P = \pi$, and I can set up the system of equations. But I am not immediately seeing a shortcut, so let me solve it directly." Interviewers can and do give hints, but only if they know where you are.

Never apologise for taking time to think. *A brief pause to structure
your approach is a sign of discipline, not weakness.*
Say: "Give me a moment to think through this" --- and mean it.

## Questions to Ask at the End

You will almost always be given five minutes to ask questions.
A candidate who asks nothing signals low engagement. And low engagement
is a reason not to extend an offer even to technically strong candidates.

Avoid questions answerable by reading the firm's website or Wikipedia.
The best questions reveal genuine intellectual curiosity about the work itself:

- *"What does the research process look like from idea generation to live deployment --- how much of that is individual versus collaborative?"*
- *"What are the hardest open problems your team is currently working on?"*
- *"What distinguishes the candidates who perform best in this role in their first year from those who take longer to find their footing?"*
- *"How does the team think about the balance between signal strength and capacity constraints when sizing positions?"*
- *"What does ongoing learning look like here --- are there reading groups, internal seminars, or regular research presentations?"*

One substantive question per interviewer is the right cadence.
If you have 30 minutes with one person, two questions is better than five.

The last question in any interview should be:

<GrayBox>

*"Is there anything from our conversation today that you would like me to clarify or expand on?"*

</GrayBox>

This is a question almost no candidate asks. It gives you a genuine second
chance on anything that landed poorly, and it signals the kind of
self-awareness and intellectual honesty that distinguishes the best candidates.
