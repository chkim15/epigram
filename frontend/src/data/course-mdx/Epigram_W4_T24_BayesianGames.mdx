# Bayesian Reasoning Under Incomplete Information

::: {.conceptbox title="Bayes-Nash Equilibrium"}
A **Bayesian game** has players with private information (types). A **Bayes-Nash equilibrium** is a strategy profile where each player maximises their expected payoff given their type *and* their belief about others' types, and these beliefs are consistent with the equilibrium strategies.

In auctions: each bidder's *type* is their private valuation $V_i$. A **bidding strategy** $b(v)$ maps valuations to bids. The equilibrium condition: for each $v$, $b(v)$ maximises expected profit given that all others bid according to $b(\cdot)$.
:::

::: {.workedbox number="1" difficulty="medium" problemid="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p32"}
**The Two Envelopes Paradox.** Two envelopes contain amounts $x$ and $2x$ where $x\sim\mathrm{Uniform}(0,100)$. You open one and see amount $Y$. Find the optimal switching strategy that maximises the probability of ending with the $2x$ envelope.
:::

::: solutionbox
**The faulty argument.** "If I see $Y$, the other envelope contains either $Y/2$ (prob 1/2) or $2Y$ (prob 1/2). Expected value if I switch: $\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{Y}{2}+\frac{1}{2}\cdot2Y=\frac{5Y}{4}>Y$. So I should always switch." This leads to an infinite regress and is *wrong* --- the probabilities are not $1/2$ each for all $Y$.

**Correct Bayesian analysis.** Given $Y=y$:

- You are holding the *small* envelope iff $x=y$, which requires $y\in(0,100)$.

- You are holding the *large* envelope iff $x=y/2$, which requires $y/2\in(0,100)$, i.e. $y\in(0,200)$.

For $y\in(0,100]$: both cases are possible. $\mathbb{P}(\text{small}\mid Y=y) = \mathbb{P}(\text{large}\mid Y=y) = \frac{1}{2}$. Switching gives the large envelope with probability $1/2$ --- no advantage.

For $y\in(100,200)$: the large envelope case requires $x=y/2\in(50,100)$ --- valid. The small envelope case requires $x=y>100$ --- impossible since $x\leq100$. **Therefore $Y>100$ implies you are holding the large envelope with certainty.**

**Optimal strategy:** $$\text{Switch if }Y\leq100;\quad\text{Keep if }Y>100$$ (Or more precisely: always switch; this gives probability $>1/2$ of ending with $2x$ because switching when $Y>100$ is suboptimal.)

Actually: if you *keep* when $Y>100$ (since you're certainly holding $2x$), and do anything when $Y\leq100$: $$\mathbb{P}(\text{end with }2x) = \mathbb{P}(Y>100)\cdot1 + \mathbb{P}(Y\leq100)\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}$$

$\mathbb{P}(Y>100)$: $Y$ is the larger envelope iff $x\in(50,100)$ (prob 1/2) and $Y=2x\in(100,200)$. So $\mathbb{P}(Y>100)=1/2$. $\mathbb{P}(Y\leq100)=1/2$.

Under the **keep-if-large strategy**: $$\mathbb{P}(\text{end with }2x) = \frac{1}{2}\cdot1 + \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2}
  = \frac{3}{4}$$

Under **always switch**: $$\mathbb{P}(\text{end with }2x)
  = \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}}_{\text{holding small, switch to large}}
  \cdot1 + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}}_{\text{holding large, switch to small}}\cdot0
  = \frac{1}{2}$$

So the optimal strategy (**keep if $Y>100$, switch otherwise**) achieves probability $3/4$ of ending with the larger amount.

**Key takeaway:** The paradox evaporates with a bounded prior. When $Y>100$, the observation reveals you are holding the larger envelope. The correct posterior uses the constraint $x\leq100$, making the probabilities asymmetric for large $Y$.
:::

::: {.freeproblem number="2" difficulty="medium" problemId="game_theory_qdsi_p1"}
**Estimating the Maximum of a Discrete Uniform Distribution.** $N\sim\mathrm{Uniform}\{1,\ldots,1000\}$. Then $X_1,\ldots,X_{10}
\stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim}\mathrm{Uniform}\{1,\ldots,N\}$. You observe the 10 numbers and guess $N$. Payoff: $+1$ if correct, $-1$ otherwise. What is the optimal strategy?
:::

::: solutionbox
**Optimal strategy:** guess $\hat{N} = M := \max(X_1,\ldots,X_{10})$.

**Proof via MAP estimation.**

The posterior given $M=m$ (the observed maximum) is: $$\mathbb{P}(N=n\mid M=m) \propto \mathbb{P}(M=m\mid N=n)\cdot\mathbb{P}(N=n)
  = \frac{m^{10}-(m-1)^{10}}{n^{10}}\cdot\frac{1}{1000}
  \propto \frac{1}{n^{10}}
  \quad\text{for }n\geq m$$ and $\mathbb{P}(N=n\mid M=m) = 0$ for $n < m$.

The posterior is *strictly decreasing* in $n$ for $n\geq m$. Therefore, the MAP (maximum a posteriori) estimate --- which maximises the posterior and hence the $+1/-1$ payoff --- is: $$\hat{N}_{\text{MAP}} = \arg\max_{n\geq m}\mathbb{P}(N=n\mid M=m) = m$$

**Intuition.** The smallest value of $N$ consistent with the data is the most likely, because smaller $N$ means the 10 draws are more concentrated (less "room" to miss a large value).

**Alternative via likelihood.** $\mathbb{P}(M=m\mid N=n) = [m/n]^{10} - [(m-1)/n]^{10}$. For $n=m$: this equals $1-[(m-1)/m]^{10}$ (the probability of at least one draw equalling $m$ in a $\{1,\ldots,m\}$ sample). For $n=m+1$: $[m/(m+1)]^{10}-[(m-1)/(m+1)]^{10}$, which is smaller. The likelihood is maximised at $n=m$.

**Is there a better strategy?**

Since the payoff is $+1$ for exact correctness and $-1$ otherwise, any guess other than the MAP is strictly worse. The MAP is optimal under the $+1/-1$ payoff rule. (Under squared error, the posterior mean $\mathbb{E}[N\mid M=m]$ would be optimal, giving a larger estimate.)

**Key takeaway:** Under $+1/-1$ payoff, the optimal guess is always the MAP (mode of the posterior). Here MAP $= M = \max(X_1,\ldots,X_{10})$. The fact that $M < N$ with positive probability does not affect the optimality --- the posterior assigns the highest mass to $N=M$.
:::

# Auction Theory

::: {.premiumproblem number="3" difficulty="hard" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p128"}
**First-Price Sealed-Bid Auction Equilibrium.** Two bidders, $V_i\stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim}\mathrm{Uniform}[0,1]$. Higher bidder wins and pays their own bid.

1.  Show the symmetric BNE strategy is $b^*(v)=v/2$.

2.  What is each bidder's expected profit?

3.  How does equilibrium change with $n$ bidders?
:::

::: solutionbox
**(a) Deriving $b^*(v) = v/2$.**

**Method: symmetric equilibrium guess-and-verify.** Suppose all bidders use a strictly increasing strategy $b(\cdot)$. Bidder 1 with value $v$ chooses bid $b$ to maximise: $$\Pi(b,v) = (v-b)\cdot\mathbb{P}(\text{win})
  = (v-b)\cdot\mathbb{P}(b^*(V_2)<b)
  = (v-b)\cdot b^{-1}(b)$$ (since $V_2 = b^{-1}(\text{opponent's bid})$ and $V_2\sim U[0,1]$, $\mathbb{P}(V_2 < b^{-1}(b)) = b^{-1}(b)$).

In equilibrium, bidder 1 optimally chooses $b=b^*(v)$, so $b^{-1}(b)=v$.

Taking first-order condition: $\frac{d}{db}[(v-b)\cdot b^{-1}(b)]=0$ and using the inverse function, we instead set up the differential equation.

**Method: differential equation.** Let $\phi = (b^*)^{-1}$, so $\phi(b)$ = type who bids $b$. FOC at equilibrium: $$\frac{d}{db}[(v-b)\phi(b)] = 0
  \implies -(b)\phi(b) + (v-b)\phi'(b) = 0
  \implies \phi'(b) = \frac{\phi(b)}{v-b}$$ Substituting $v = b/\phi(b)\cdot\phi(b) + b = b$ at the symmetric point\...

**Cleaner: directly assume $b^*(v) = \alpha v$ and find $\alpha$.**

If all bidders use $b_j = \alpha V_j$: $$\Pi(\alpha v, v) = (v - \alpha v)\cdot\mathbb{P}(\alpha V_2 < \alpha v)
  = v(1-\alpha)\cdot v = v^2(1-\alpha)$$ Bidder 1 wants to optimise over their actual bid $b$: $$\Pi(b,v) = (v-b)\cdot\mathbb{P}(\alpha V_2 < b)
  = (v-b)\cdot\frac{b}{\alpha}$$ FOC in $b$: $\frac{d}{db}[(v-b)\frac{b}{\alpha}] = \frac{1}{\alpha}[v-2b]=0
\implies b^*= v/2$, so $\alpha = 1/2$.

$$\boxed{b^*(v) = \frac{v}{2}}$$

**(b) Expected profit.** $$\Pi(v) = (v - v/2)\cdot\mathbb{P}(V_2/2 < v/2)
  = \frac{v}{2}\cdot v = \frac{v^2}{2}$$ Ex-ante expected profit (integrating over $v\sim U[0,1]$): $$\mathbb{E}[\Pi] = \int_0^1 \frac{v^2}{2}\,dv = \frac{1}{6}$$

**(c) General $n$ bidders.**

The symmetric BNE with $n$ bidders, $V_i\stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim}U[0,1]$: $$\boxed{b^*(v) = \frac{n-1}{n}\,v}$$

**Intuition:** With more competition, each bidder shades their bid less (bids closer to true value) to avoid losing. As $n\to\infty$, $b^*(v)\to v$ (converges to truthful bidding, as in the second-price auction).

Expected revenue: $\mathbb{E}[P^{(1)}_{(n)}]=\frac{n-1}{n}\cdot\frac{n}{n+1}
=\frac{n-1}{n+1}$ where $P^{(1)}_{(n)}$ is the first-order statistic. For $n=2$: $\mathbb{E}[\text{rev}]=1/3$.

**Key takeaway:** First-price BNE = bid $(n-1)/n$ of true value. More bidders $\Rightarrow$ less shading $\Rightarrow$ higher revenue.
:::

::: {.premiumproblem number="4" difficulty="hard" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p129"}
**Revenue Equivalence Theorem.** $n$ bidders, i.i.d. private values from $U[0,1]$.

1.  State the Revenue Equivalence Theorem.

2.  Equilibrium strategy in the second-price (Vickrey) auction.

3.  Conditions for revenue equivalence.

4.  Example where revenue equivalence fails.
:::

::: solutionbox
**(a) Revenue Equivalence Theorem (RET).**

*Any two auction mechanisms that (i) always allocate the object to the highest-value bidder, and (ii) give the same expected utility to the lowest-type bidder (typically zero), generate the same expected payment rule and therefore the same expected seller revenue.*

Under the IPV model with $n$ risk-neutral bidders and values $V_i\stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim}U[0,1]$, the following four formats all yield the same expected revenue: first-price sealed-bid, second-price (Vickrey), English, and all-pay. $$\mathbb{E}[\text{revenue}] = \mathbb{E}[V_{(n-1:n)}] = \frac{n-1}{n+1}$$ For $n=2$: $\mathbb{E}[\text{rev}]=1/3$. For $n=3$: $1/2$. For $n=4$: $3/5$.

**(b) Second-price (Vickrey) auction: dominant strategy.**

The winner pays the *second-highest* bid. Bidding your true value $b_i = v_i$ is a **weakly dominant strategy**.

*Why:* Your own bid affects whether you win but *not* the price you pay when you win (price $=$ highest competing bid). So your best strategy is simply to win exactly when winning is profitable --- i.e. when the highest rival bid is below your value $v$. This is achieved by bidding $v$ truthfully: $$\boxed{b^*(v) = v}$$ Overbidding can cause you to win at a loss; underbidding can cause you to miss profitable wins. Both are weakly dominated by truthful bidding.

**(c) Conditions for revenue equivalence.**

1.  **Independent private values (IPV):** each bidder knows their own value and it is independent of others' values.

2.  **Risk neutrality:** bidders maximise expected payoff.

3.  **Symmetric bidders:** values drawn i.i.d. from the same distribution.

4.  **Efficient allocation:** the object always goes to the highest-value bidder.

5.  **Equal participation payoff for the lowest type:** the bidder with value $v=0$ earns zero expected surplus.

6.  **No binding budget constraints or distortions:** bidders can make required payments; no reserve prices or entry asymmetries (unless applied consistently).

**(d) When RET fails: risk-averse bidders.**

**First-price vs second-price with risk-averse bidders:**

- In a **second-price** auction, truthful bidding remains optimal even with risk aversion (the dominant strategy argument does not rely on risk neutrality).

- In a **first-price** auction, a risk-averse bidder dislikes the uncertainty of losing. They bid more aggressively than a risk-neutral bidder --- shading less below their true value --- to reduce the probability of losing.

- Result: first-price revenue $>$ second-price revenue under risk aversion.

Other failures of RET:

- **Common-value / correlated values** (e.g. oil field auctions): the English auction raises more revenue than sealed-bid formats (Milgrom--Weber, 1982).

- **Asymmetric bidder distributions**.

- **Reserve prices, entry costs, or budget constraints**.

**Key takeaway:** RET says all "standard" auction formats are revenue-equivalent under IPV, risk neutrality, symmetry, and efficiency. Second-price $\Rightarrow$ bid truthfully (dominant strategy). First-price $\Rightarrow$ shade to $(n-1)/n\cdot v$. Both give $\mathbb{E}[\text{rev}]=(n-1)/(n+1)$. RET breaks most commonly via risk aversion (raises first-price revenue) or correlated values (raises English auction revenue).
:::

# Technique Summary

::: {.techniquesummary title="Topic 24 Technique Toolkit"}

| Technique | Description |
|---|---|
| **Two envelopes** | With bounded prior ($x\leq M$): $Y>M$ reveals you hold the large amount. Keep it. Optimal $\mathbb{P}(2x)=3/4$. |
| **MAP estimation** | Under $+1/-1$ payoff: guess the posterior mode. For max of $U\{1,\ldots,N\}$: MAP $=$ observed maximum. |
| **First-price BNE** | $b^*(v)=\frac{n-1}{n}v$. More bidders $\Rightarrow$ less shading. |
| **Second-price BNE** | Bid $b_i=v_i$ (truthful bidding is weakly dominant). |
| **Revenue Equivalence** | $\mathbb{E}[\text{rev}]=E[V_{(n-1:n)}]=\frac{n-1}{n+1}$ for $U[0,1]$, $n$ bidders. Holds under private values, symmetry, risk neutrality. |
| **RET fails when** | Risk aversion (raises first-price rev), correlated values, or resale/budget constraints. |

:::
