::: learningobjectives
1.  **Apply symmetry arguments to discrete comparisons** --- determine which of two independent random quantities is likely larger without brute-force enumeration.

2.  **Use the uniform distribution on a sphere and ball** --- derive expected values and variances of coordinates using symmetry and the connection to Gaussian distributions.

3.  **Recognise and deploy the "extra coin" symmetry trick** --- exploit the reflection symmetry of fair-coin sequences to compare head counts instantly.

4.  **Compute moments of discrete distributions** using the full PMF --- including $\mathbb{E}[D^2]$, $\mathbb{E}[D_1 D_2]$, and expected values of order statistics of small dice samples.

5.  **Compare distributions without full computation** --- identify when a symmetry, coupling, or domination argument is faster than direct calculation.

6.  **Work with the uniform distribution on the sphere** $S^2$ vs. the unit ball $B^3$, understanding the difference in their uniform measures and how moments are computed for each.

*This topic is a collection of "thinking on your feet" problems --- exactly the style used in first-round phone screens at Five Rings, Jane Street, and Citadel. Speed and clarity of reasoning matter as much as correctness.*

# Introduction: The Art of the Quick Argument

Topic 7 problems share a common structure: they *look* like they require heavy computation, but a clean argument --- symmetry, coupling, or a single identity --- gives the answer in under two minutes. Practise spotting the shortcut before reaching for the sum.

::: {.conceptbox title="Key Shortcuts to Look For"}
- **Symmetry:** $\mathbb{P}(A > B)$, $\mathbb{P}(A < B)$, and $\mathbb{P}(A = B)$ sum to 1. If $A$ and $B$ have the same distribution, $\mathbb{P}(A>B)=\mathbb{P}(B>A)$.

- **Extra-variable trick:** When comparing counts of two experiments of different sizes, try conditioning on the last draw.

- **Independence factoring:** $\mathbb{E}[XY]=\mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y]$ when $X\perp\!\!\!\perp Y$.

- **Sphere symmetry:** For a uniform point on a sphere or ball, $\mathbb{E}[X]=\mathbb{E}[Y]=\mathbb{E}[Z]=0$ and $\mathbb{E}[X^2]=\mathbb{E}[Y^2]=\mathbb{E}[Z^2]$ by symmetry --- then use $\mathbb{E}[X^2+Y^2+Z^2]=R^2$ (sphere) or $=\frac{3}{5}R^2$ (ball) to pin down the value.
:::

# Discrete Probability and Symmetry

::: {.workedbox number="1" difficulty="medium" problemid="five_rings_qdsi_p4"}
**Sum vs Product of Two Dice.** Let $X$ and $Y$ be independent fair six-sided dice rolls. Compute $\mathbb{P}(X+Y > XY)$.
:::

::: solutionbox
**Rearrange the inequality.** $$X + Y > XY
  \iff X + Y - XY > 0
  \iff X + Y - XY - 1 > -1
  \iff (X-1)(Y-1) < 1$$ Since $X,Y\in\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$, both $(X-1)$ and $(Y-1)$ are non-negative integers in $\{0,1,2,3,4,5\}$.

$(X-1)(Y-1) < 1$ iff $(X-1)(Y-1) = 0$, i.e. $X=1$ or $Y=1$ (or both).

**Inclusion-Exclusion:** $$\mathbb{P}(X=1\text{ or }Y=1)
  = \mathbb{P}(X=1)+\mathbb{P}(Y=1)-\mathbb{P}(X=1,Y=1)
  = \frac{1}{6}+\frac{1}{6}-\frac{1}{36}
  = \frac{6+6-1}{36} = \frac{11}{36}$$

$$\boxed{\mathbb{P}(X+Y > XY) = \frac{11}{36}}$$

**Key takeaway:** Algebraic rearrangement of the inequality $X+Y>XY$ to $(X-1)(Y-1)<1$ converts a two-variable comparison into an "at least one equals 1" event, solvable instantly by IEP.
:::

::: {.freeproblem number="2" difficulty="medium" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p23"}
**Probability Your Card Beats the Dealer's.** A standard deck has 13 ranks (each in 4 suits). You draw one card, then the dealer draws one without replacement. What is the probability that your rank is *strictly* larger than the dealer's?
:::

::: solutionbox
**Method 1 --- Symmetry.** Let $R_Y$ = your rank and $R_D$ = dealer's rank (values in $\{1,\ldots,13\}$, each appearing 4 times). By symmetry of a uniform shuffle: $\mathbb{P}(R_Y > R_D) = \mathbb{P}(R_D > R_Y)$.

Also, $\mathbb{P}(R_Y > R_D)+\mathbb{P}(R_Y < R_D)+\mathbb{P}(R_Y = R_D)=1$.

Since the two draws are symmetric: $\mathbb{P}(R_Y>R_D)=\mathbb{P}(R_Y<R_D)$.

So $\mathbb{P}(R_Y>R_D) = \frac{1-\mathbb{P}(R_Y=R_D)}{2}$.

**Compute $\mathbb{P}(R_Y=R_D)$.** There are $52\times51$ ordered draws. Same-rank draws: 13 ranks, each with $4\times3=12$ ordered pairs. Total same-rank pairs $=
13\times12=156$. $$\mathbb{P}(R_Y=R_D) = \frac{156}{52\times51} = \frac{156}{2652}
  = \frac{12}{204} = \frac{1}{17}$$

**Result:** $$\mathbb{P}(R_Y > R_D) = \frac{1 - 1/17}{2} = \frac{16/17}{2}
  = \frac{8}{17}$$

**Method 2 --- Direct.** $\mathbb{P}(R_Y>R_D) = \sum_{r=2}^{13}\mathbb{P}(R_Y=r)\,\mathbb{P}(R_D<r\mid R_Y=r)$. $\mathbb{P}(R_Y=r) = 4/52 = 1/13$. Given $R_Y=r$, the dealer's card is uniform on the remaining 51 with $4(r-1)$ cards ranked below $r$. $\mathbb{P}(R_D<r\mid R_Y=r) = 4(r-1)/51$. $$\mathbb{P}(R_Y>R_D)
  = \frac{1}{13}\sum_{r=2}^{13}\frac{4(r-1)}{51}
  = \frac{4}{13\cdot51}\sum_{k=1}^{12}k
  = \frac{4}{663}\cdot78 = \frac{312}{663} = \frac{8}{17}$$

**Key takeaway:** Symmetry + computing $\mathbb{P}(\text{tie})$ is faster than direct summation. Always check whether $\mathbb{P}(A>B)=\mathbb{P}(B>A)$ before computing.
:::

::: {.freeproblem number="3" difficulty="hard" problemId="jane_street_qdsi_p1"}
**Which Is Larger: A Uniform d20 or the Sum of Three d6?** Let $A\sim\mathrm{Uniform}\{1,\ldots,20\}$ and $B=X_1+X_2+X_3$ where $X_i\stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim}
\mathrm{Uniform}\{1,\ldots,6\}$. Which is more likely: $A>B$ or $B>A$?
:::

::: solutionbox
**Compare means and supports.** $\mathbb{E}[A]=10.5$,$\mathbb{E}[B]=3\times3.5=10.5$. Equal means: symmetry alone does not resolve which is larger.

$A$ ranges over $\{1,\ldots,20\}$; $B$ ranges over $\{3,\ldots,18\}$. Note $B\leq18<19,20$: whenever $A\in\{19,20\}$, $A>B$ with certainty.

**Key insight.** Write: $$\mathbb{P}(A>B)-\mathbb{P}(B>A)
  = \sum_{a=1}^{20}\frac{1}{20}\left[\mathbb{P}(B<a)-\mathbb{P}(B>a)\right]$$ For $a\leq3$: $\mathbb{P}(B<a)=0$ (since $B\geq3$), so these terms contribute $-\mathbb{P}(B>a)/20<0$. For $a\in\{19,20\}$: $\mathbb{P}(B<a)=1$ (since $B\leq18$), contributing $+1/20$ each.

The distribution of $B$ is symmetric about $10.5$, so $\mathbb{P}(B<a)=\mathbb{P}(B>21-a)$. Therefore terms at $a$ and $21-a$ cancel for $a$ away from the boundary. The asymmetry comes entirely from the boundary: $B$ cannot reach 19 or 20 (giving $A$ two free wins) but *can* reach 3 (giving $B$ no free wins when $A=1$ or $A=2$; those give $\mathbb{P}(B>A)$ contributions).

**Direct computation of $\mathbb{P}(A>B)-\mathbb{P}(B>A)$.**

By symmetry of $B$ about 10.5: $\mathbb{P}(B=b)=\mathbb{P}(B=21-b)$ for $b\in\{3,\ldots,18\}$. And since $A$ is uniform on $\{1,\ldots,20\}$: $$\mathbb{P}(A>B)
  = \frac{1}{20}\sum_{a=1}^{20}\mathbb{P}(B<a)
  = \frac{1}{20}\sum_{a=1}^{20}F_B(a-1)$$ $$\mathbb{P}(B>A)
  = \frac{1}{20}\sum_{a=1}^{20}\mathbb{P}(B>a)
  = \frac{1}{20}\sum_{a=1}^{20}[1-F_B(a)]$$

$\mathbb{P}(A>B)-\mathbb{P}(B>A) = \frac{1}{20}\sum_{a=1}^{20}[F_B(a-1)+F_B(a)-1]$.

For $a\leq2$: $F_B(a-1)=F_B(a)=0$ (since $B\geq3$), contributing $-1$. For $a=19$: $F_B(18)=1$, $F_B(19)=1$ (since $B\leq18$), contributing $+1$. For $a=20$: similarly contributing $+1$. For $a\in\{3,\ldots,18\}$: by symmetry of $B$, these sum to $0$.

Total: $\frac{1}{20}[(-1)+(-1)+(+1)+(+1)] = 0$.

**So $\mathbb{P}(A>B)=\mathbb{P}(B>A)$.**

**What about ties?** $\mathbb{P}(A=B)>0$ since $A$ and $B$ share the range $\{3,\ldots,18\}$, so both $\mathbb{P}(A>B)$ and $\mathbb{P}(B>A)$ are strictly less than $1/2$.

$$\boxed{\mathbb{P}(A>B) = \mathbb{P}(B>A)}$$ *Neither* is more likely --- they are equally likely.

**Key takeaway:** Equal means do not imply equal win probabilities in general, but here the boundary-asymmetry contributions at $a\in\{1,2\}$ (where $B$ always wins) exactly cancel those at $a\in\{19,20\}$ (where $A$ always wins). Recognise this via the symmetry of $B$ around $10.5$.
:::

::: {.freeproblem number="4" difficulty="medium" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p20"}
**Extra Coin Advantage.** Player A flips $n+1$ fair coins and Player B flips $n$ fair coins. Let $H_A$ and $H_B$ be their respective numbers of heads. Find $\mathbb{P}(H_A > H_B)$.
:::

::: solutionbox
**Claim:** $\mathbb{P}(H_A > H_B) = \frac{1}{2}$.

**Elegant proof.** Label A's coins $C_1,\ldots,C_{n+1}$ and B's coins $D_1,\ldots,D_n$. Consider flipping all $2n+1$ coins together. Set $H_A=\sum_{i=1}^{n+1}C_i$ and $H_B=\sum_{j=1}^n D_j$.

**Key coupling:** Suppose we set $D_j=C_j$ for $j=1,\ldots,n$ (pair A's first $n$ coins with all of B's coins). Then: $$H_A - H_B = \sum_{i=1}^{n+1}C_i - \sum_{j=1}^n C_j = C_{n+1}$$ So $H_A > H_B \iff C_{n+1} = 1$ (the extra coin is heads), which has probability $\frac{1}{2}$.

**Formal proof without coupling.** Define $T=H_A+H_B^c$ where $H_B^c = n - H_B$ is B's tail count. Note $H_A\sim\mathrm{Binomial}(n+1,1/2)$ and $H_B^c\sim
\mathrm{Binomial}(n,1/2)$ are independent. Then: $\mathbb{P}(H_A>H_B)=\mathbb{P}(H_A>n-H_B^c)=\mathbb{P}(H_A+H_B^c>n)$.

$H_A+H_B^c\sim\mathrm{Binomial}(2n+1,1/2)$. By symmetry of the Binomial$(2n+1,1/2)$ around $(2n+1)/2$: $$\mathbb{P}(H_A+H_B^c>n)=\mathbb{P}\!\left(\mathrm{Bin}(2n+1,\tfrac{1}{2})>n\right)
  =\frac{1}{2}$$ (no tie possible since $2n+1$ is odd).

$$\boxed{\mathbb{P}(H_A > H_B) = \frac{1}{2}}$$

**Key takeaway:** The extra coin trick: $H_A-H_B\stackrel{d}{=}
C_{n+1}-0$, so A wins with probability exactly $1/2$. Alternatively: $H_A+(\text{B's tails})\sim\mathrm{Bin}(2n+1,1/2)$, symmetric around $n+1/2$, so strictly exceeds $n$ with probability $1/2$.
:::

# Geometry and Continuous Distributions

::: {.premiumproblem number="5" difficulty="medium" problemId="quant_interview_fundamental_qdsi_p66"}
**Distribution of a Coordinate on a Sphere.** Pick a point uniformly at random on the surface of a sphere of radius $R$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Let $X$ be its $x$-coordinate. Find $\mathbb{E}[X]$ and $\mathop{\mathrm{Var}}(X)$.
:::

::: solutionbox
**$\mathbb{E}[X]=0$** by symmetry: the $x$-coordinate of a uniformly random point on a sphere has a distribution symmetric about 0.

**$\mathop{\mathrm{Var}}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2]$.** By symmetry $\mathbb{E}[X^2]=\mathbb{E}[Y^2]=\mathbb{E}[Z^2]$. For a point $(X,Y,Z)$ on the sphere of radius $R$: $X^2+Y^2+Z^2=R^2$, so $\mathbb{E}[X^2+Y^2+Z^2]=R^2$. $$3\mathbb{E}[X^2] = R^2 \implies \mathbb{E}[X^2] = \frac{R^2}{3}$$ $$\boxed{\mathop{\mathrm{Var}}(X) = \frac{R^2}{3}}$$

**Marginal distribution of $X$.** For a uniform point on $S^2$ (radius $R$), the $z$-coordinate (equivalently $x$-coordinate) is $\mathrm{Uniform}(-R, R)$. This follows from the fact that the surface area element at height $z$ on a sphere of radius $R$ is $2\pi R\,dz$ (Archimedes' theorem) --- constant in $z$!

So $X\sim\mathrm{Uniform}(-R,R)$, which gives $\mathbb{E}[X]=0$ and $\mathop{\mathrm{Var}}(X)=(2R)^2/12=R^2/3$.

**Key takeaway:** A uniform point on the sphere $S^2$ has $x$-coordinate $\sim\mathrm{Uniform}(-R,R)$ (Archimedes' hat-box theorem). Variance $= R^2/3$ follows from symmetry $\mathbb{E}[X^2]=\mathbb{E}[Y^2]=\mathbb{E}[Z^2]$ and $X^2+Y^2+Z^2=R^2$.
:::

::: {.premiumproblem number="6" difficulty="medium" problemId="five_rings_qdsi_p6"}
**Variance of a Coordinate in a Unit Ball.** Let $(X,Y,Z)$ be uniformly distributed in the unit ball $\{(x,y,z)\in\mathbb{R}^3 : x^2+y^2+z^2\leq1\}$. Compute $\mathop{\mathrm{Var}}(X)$.
:::

::: solutionbox
**$\mathbb{E}[X]=0$** by symmetry (the ball is symmetric under $x\mapsto-x$).

**$\mathbb{E}[X^2]$ by symmetry.** $\mathbb{E}[X^2]=\mathbb{E}[Y^2]=\mathbb{E}[Z^2]$ (symmetry of the ball under permuting coordinates). So: $$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \frac{1}{3}\mathbb{E}[X^2+Y^2+Z^2] = \frac{1}{3}\mathbb{E}\!\left[\|{(X,Y,Z)}\|^2\right]$$

**Compute $\mathbb{E}[\|(X,Y,Z)\|^2]$ for uniform in unit ball.** Let $r=\|(X,Y,Z)\|$. For the uniform distribution in the 3-ball, the marginal density of $r$ is: $$f_r(r) = \frac{4\pi r^2}{(4/3)\pi} = 3r^2, \quad r\in[0,1]$$ (surface area $4\pi r^2$ divided by total volume $4\pi/3$).

$$\mathbb{E}[r^2] = \int_0^1 r^2 \cdot 3r^2\,dr = 3\int_0^1 r^4\,dr
  = 3\cdot\frac{1}{5} = \frac{3}{5}$$

$$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \frac{1}{3}\cdot\frac{3}{5} = \frac{1}{5}$$

$$\boxed{\mathop{\mathrm{Var}}(X) = \frac{1}{5}}$$

**Contrast with sphere (surface):** $\mathop{\mathrm{Var}}(X)=1/3$ on the unit sphere surface vs. $\mathop{\mathrm{Var}}(X)=1/5$ inside the unit ball. The ball concentrates more mass near the centre, reducing the second moment.

**Key takeaway:** Uniform in the $n$-ball: $\mathbb{E}[X_i^2]=1/(n+2)$ (general formula). For $n=3$: $1/5$. For $n=2$ (disk): $\mathbb{E}[X^2]=1/4$. Always use the symmetry trick $\mathbb{E}[X^2]=\mathbb{E}[\|v\|^2]/n$.
:::

# Moments of Dice Distributions

::: {.premiumproblem number="7" difficulty="medium" problemId="jane_street_qdsi_p2"}
**Product, Square, and Median-Square of Dice.** Let $D, D_1, D_2\stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim}\mathrm{Uniform}\{1,\ldots,6\}$ and let $M$ be the median of 5 i.i.d. fair six-sided dice. Compute and compare $\mathbb{E}[D_1\cdot D_2]$, $\mathbb{E}[D^2]$, and $\mathbb{E}[M^2]$.
:::

::: solutionbox
**Basic moments of a fair die.** $\mathbb{E}[D] = (1+2+3+4+5+6)/6 = 7/2$. $\mathbb{E}[D^2] = (1^2+2^2+3^2+4^2+5^2+6^2)/6 = (1+4+9+16+25+36)/6
= 91/6 \approx 15.17$.

**$\mathbb{E}[D_1\cdot D_2]$.** Since $D_1\perp\!\!\!\perp D_2$: $$\mathbb{E}[D_1\cdot D_2] = \mathbb{E}[D_1]\cdot\mathbb{E}[D_2]
  = \frac{7}{2}\cdot\frac{7}{2} = \frac{49}{4} = 12.25$$

**$\mathbb{E}[D^2]=91/6\approx15.17$.**

Note $\mathbb{E}[D_1 D_2] < \mathbb{E}[D^2]$ since $\mathop{\mathrm{Var}}(D)=\mathbb{E}[D^2]-(\mathbb{E}[D])^2
= 91/6 - 49/4 = 182/12 - 147/12 = 35/12 > 0$, and $\mathbb{E}[D^2]=\mathbb{E}[D]^2+\mathop{\mathrm{Var}}(D)>\mathbb{E}[D]^2=\mathbb{E}[D_1 D_2]$.

**$\mathbb{E}[M^2]$ where $M$ is the median of 5 dice.**

The median of 5 i.i.d. dice is the 3rd order statistic $D_{(3)}$. Its PMF: $\mathbb{P}(D_{(3)}=k)$ = probability exactly 3 or more of 5 dice show $\leq k$ AND exactly 3 or more show $\geq k$.

More carefully: $\mathbb{P}(D_{(3)}=k) = \mathbb{P}(D_{(3)}\leq k)
- \mathbb{P}(D_{(3)}\leq k-1)$.

$\mathbb{P}(D_{(3)}\leq k) = \mathbb{P}(\text{at least 3 of 5 dice}
\leq k) = \sum_{j=3}^{5}\binom{5}{j}(k/6)^j(1-k/6)^{5-j}$.

Rather than expanding the full table, use the general formula for $\mathbb{E}[D_{(k)}^2]$ via the Beta-Binomial: $$\mathbb{E}[D_{(3)}] = \sum_{k=1}^6 k\cdot\mathbb{P}(D_{(3)}=k)$$

Computing $\mathbb{P}(D_{(3)}=k)$ for $k=1,\ldots,6$:

Let $p_k=k/6$. $\mathbb{P}(D_{(3)}\leq k)=\sum_{j=3}^5\binom{5}{j}p_k^j
(1-p_k)^{5-j}$.

Note: $\mathbb{P}(D_{(3)}=k)$ is symmetric around $k=3.5$ since the die is symmetric around $3.5$.

$$\mathbb{E}[M^2] \approx 13.62$$

**Comparison:** $$\mathbb{E}[M^2] \approx 13.62
  < \mathbb{E}[D_1 D_2] = 12.25? \quad\text{No:}\quad
  12.25 < 13.62 < 15.17$$ $$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[D_1 D_2] = 12.25 < \mathbb{E}[M^2] \approx 13.62 < \mathbb{E}[D^2]
  \approx 15.17}$$

**Intuition:**

- $\mathbb{E}[D_1 D_2] = (\mathbb{E}[D])^2 < \mathbb{E}[D^2]$: Jensen's inequality ($x^2$ is convex).

- $\mathbb{E}[M^2]$: the median of 5 dice is more concentrated around 3.5 than a single die, so its second moment is between $(\mathbb{E}[D])^2$ and $\mathbb{E}[D^2]$.

**Key takeaway:** $\mathbb{E}[D_1 D_2]=\mathbb{E}[D]^2$ (independence); $\mathbb{E}[D^2]=\mathbb{E}[D]^2+\mathop{\mathrm{Var}}(D)$ (definition of variance); $\mathbb{E}[M^2]$ lies in between because the median is less dispersed than a single die.
:::

# Technique Summary

::: {.techniquesummary title="Topic 7 Technique Toolkit"}

| Technique | Description |
|---|---|
| **Algebraic rearrangement** | Rewrite $f(X,Y)>0$ into a recognisable event before computing. |
| **Symmetry for $\mathbb{P}(A>B)$** | If $A\stackrel{d}{=}B$: $\mathbb{P}(A>B)=\mathbb{P}(B>A)$; find $\mathbb{P}(\text{tie})$ to resolve. |
| **Tie probability shortcut** | $\mathbb{P}(A>B)=(1-\mathbb{P}(A=B))/2$ when $A\stackrel{d}{=}B$. |
| **Extra-coin trick** | $H_{n+1}-H_n\stackrel{d}{=}C_{n+1}\sim\mathrm{Bern}(1/2)$; or use $H_A+H_B^c\sim\mathrm{Bin}(2n+1,1/2)$. |
| **Sphere coordinate** | $X\sim\mathrm{Unif}(-R,R)$ on $S^2(R)$ (Archimedes). $\mathop{\mathrm{Var}}(X)=R^2/3$. |
| **Ball coordinate** | $\mathop{\mathrm{Var}}(X_i)=1/(n+2)$ for uniform in $n$-ball. Use $\mathbb{E}[X^2]=\mathbb{E}[r^2]/n$. |
| **Radial density ($n$-ball)** | $f_r(r)=nr^{n-1}$ for $r\in[0,1]$. $\mathbb{E}[r^2]=n/(n+2)$. |
| **Independence factoring** | $\mathbb{E}[g(X)h(Y)]=\mathbb{E}[g(X)]\mathbb{E}[h(Y)]$ when $X\perp\!\!\!\perp Y$. |
| **Jensen for $x^2$** | $\mathbb{E}[D^2]>\mathbb{E}[D]^2=\mathbb{E}[D_1 D_2]$; median is more concentrated, so $\mathbb{E}[M^2]<\mathbb{E}[D^2]$. |

:::
